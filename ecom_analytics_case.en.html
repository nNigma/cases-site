Documentation: CDI / BDI

CDI (Category Development Index) and BDI (Brand Development Index) are indices of "strong" and "weak" territories/segments relative to the average. They are handy for finding growth zones, prioritizing marketing/distribution and planning offline presence.

Intuition (in plain words)

  • CDI answers: "How developed is the category here relative to the average?"
  • BDI answers: "How strong is the brand/product here relative to the average?"
  • We compare not absolute sales but a normalized level (e.g. per person or per audience).

Formulas (basic)

CDI(region) = 100 * ( CategorySales(region) / Population(region) )
                    / ( CategorySales(total)  / Population(total) )

BDI(region) = 100 * ( BrandSales(region) / Population(region) )
                    / ( BrandSales(total)  / Population(total) )

In practice, instead of Population you can use any available "base" - audience, households, active buyers, number of receipts, etc. What matters is that the denominator is comparable across regions.

Calculation methodology (steps)

  • Pick a "normalization unit": population/audience/households/buyers.
  • Define the period: week/month/quarter, and consistent filters.
  • Agree the category definition: categorization rules, dictionary, versions.
  • Compute sales/volume by region and overall.
  • Normalize to the base (per 1 person, 1 buyer, etc.).
  • Index against the overall average (×100).
  • Segment regions into quadrants (see below).

How to interpret (quadrants)

  • CDI > 100, BDI > 100: strong category and strong brand → retain/scale.
  • CDI > 100, BDI < 100: developed category, weak brand → attack zone/market share.
  • CDI < 100, BDI > 100: brand stronger than category → niche lead, grow carefully.
  • CDI < 100, BDI < 100: weak zone → minimal support or "long game".

Practical tip: for robustness add volume thresholds (e.g. minimum receipts/sales), so the index isn't "skewed" on small numbers.

Documentation: maps for choosing an offline location

The map helps choose a district/location to open an offline point, accounting for routes, population and potential demand. Note: the map is a prioritization tool; the final decision is backed by field checks (competitors, rent, traffic by time of day, constraints).

What is considered

  • Routes/movement (how people actually move around the city)
  • Population density and distribution across districts
  • Overlap with the existing network (where points/partners already are)
  • "Coverage zone" scenarios (radii, isochrones, "time-to-point")

Methodology (simplified)

  • Split the city into cells/districts (geo-grid or administrative borders).
  • Estimate potential: population × demand/activity coefficients.
  • Add routes: where the flow of people "passes through", not only "lives".
  • Remove overlap: don't open where a zone is already covered by existing points.
  • Rank districts by an integral score and explain the factor contributions.
